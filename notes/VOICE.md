# VOICE.md

## Overview

The voice of *The End of Inside* should reflect a world where:

- understanding is immediate
- language is secondary
- silence carries meaning

The prose should be:

- clean
- restrained
- precise
- easy to move through

Avoid:

- over-explaining
- heavy exposition
- decorative language
- density that becomes exhausting

Favor:

- clarity
- emotional accuracy
- subtle shifts in perception
- breathable paragraph and scene structure

Depth should not come from piling observation on top of observation until the prose becomes hard to move through. The writing can remain lyrical, intelligent, and emotionally layered while still being structurally open. A reader should feel carried forward through the scene, not asked to push through it.

---

## Narrative Voice

### Style

- Close third-person, primarily following Rei
- Minimal filtering (“she felt,” “she thought” should be used sparingly)
- Sensory-emotional descriptions over intellectual analysis
- Prefer one clear motion, observation, or emotional turn per paragraph when possible

The narration should feel:

- present
- grounded
- slightly detached at times

Especially early on, Rei’s perspective lacks strong boundaries.

This should be reflected in:

- blended perceptions
- ambiguous ownership of thoughts and feelings

Fluidity does not require maximal density. Let permeability appear through carefully chosen details, clear paragraphing, and steady forward movement. The effect should feel immersive, not crowded.

---

### Evolution of Narrative Voice

**Act I — Fluid**
- thoughts and emotions blend freely
- unclear distinctions between Rei and others
- sentences may drift slightly without sharp edges

**Act II — Disrupted**
- sharper, more fragmented
- isolation introduces harsh internal clarity
- more explicit “I vs other” awareness begins to form

**Act III — Intentional**
- precise and controlled
- clear distinctions between self and other
- language becomes more deliberate

---

## Dialogue Philosophy

Dialogue is not the primary vehicle of communication.

Characters often:

- say less than they know
- rely on shared understanding
- leave things unfinished

Important:

> What is not said matters as much as what is said.

Silence, pauses, and withheld connection are meaningful.

When the past is explained, do not let explanation become closure. Let witnesses remember the same event in slightly incompatible shapes. The scene should appear to clarify and actually deepen uncertainty.

When a scene needs to feel slightly wrong, do it through realistic misalignment rather than emphasis. Use continued gaze, proximity that does not correct itself, over-precise observations, premature familiarity, and pauses that fail to close. Keep the tone calm. Do not label the unease for the reader.

When quiet escalation is needed, increase it through missed cues, repeated actions, language that returns slightly altered, and a person taking one beat too long to rejoin the scene. Build a spectrum rather than making everyone react at the same intensity.

# Character Voice Direction

## **Rei Nakamura**

### Core Traits

- open
- gentle
- perceptive
- lacks instinct for separation (early)

---

### Act I Voice

Rei speaks:

- simply
- without defensiveness
- without hesitation

Late in Act I, the first hairline change can appear:

- she may almost leave something unsaid
- she may try, too late, to make a sentence smaller after speaking it

She does not:

- assert herself strongly
- question emotional blending
- distinguish clearly between perspectives

Her dialogue often feels:

- inclusive
- soft
- slightly unanchored

Example tone:

> “It’s okay. I’m here.”  
> “I think… we’re the same right now.”

---

### Act II Voice

Rei becomes:

- uncertain
- more self-aware
- slightly destabilized

She begins to:

- question what belongs to her
- hesitate before connecting
- search for language to describe separation

Her speech includes:

- pauses
- corrections
- unfinished thoughts

Example tone:

> “Wait… was that me?”  
> “I don’t know where that started.”

---

### Act III Voice

Rei becomes:

- precise
- intentional
- calm, but not detached

She chooses:

- when to connect
- how deeply to connect
- what to withhold

Her voice is:

- measured
- respectful of boundaries
- quietly authoritative

Example tone:

> “I can feel you. But I’m staying here.”  
> “I understand. I’m not going to take it from you.”

---

## **Yui Takahashi**

### Core Traits

- warm
- curious
- emotionally receptive
- vulnerable to influence

---

### Early Voice

Yui speaks:

- openly
- with curiosity
- with emotional immediacy
- around Rei, she may move a little too quickly past ordinary social buffers
- in messages or quick exchanges, her replies may seem to arrive before the previous beat has fully settled

She is comfortable expressing:

- confusion
- excitement
- closeness

In early scenes with Rei, Yui’s dialogue can occasionally feel a shade too precise or too familiar for the amount of time that has passed. This should remain plausible, understated, and slightly off rather than dramatic.

Example tone:

> “That felt… really clear.”  
> “Is it always like this with you?”

---

### Mid Voice (Destabilization)

As Yui is affected by Rei:

- her speech becomes uncertain
- identity confusion appears
- emotional tone fluctuates

She may:

- contradict herself
- lose track of what she’s saying
- ask grounding questions

Example tone:

> “Wait, I thought—no, that wasn’t…”  
> “I don’t know if that’s mine.”

---

### Late Voice

After recovery begins:

- slower
- more cautious
- more aware of boundaries

She speaks with:

- care
- slight hesitation
- regained sense of self

Example tone:

> “Can we go slower?”  
> “I want to stay… me.”

---

## **Suzu Sato (Second Bell)**

### Core Traits

- controlled
- disciplined
- austere
- anti-theatrical
- emotionally guarded
- deeply shaped by past trauma

---

### General Voice

Suzu speaks:

- directly
- with almost no social grease
- without embellishment
- without emotional leakage

She avoids:

- vulnerability
- unnecessary explanation
- overheated condemnation
- emotional merging

Her language is:

- precise
- restrained
- deliberate
- unnervingly reasonable

Example tone:

> “You’re causing harm.”  
> “This was necessary.”

---

### Emotional Underlayer

Though controlled, Suzu is not empty.

Her restraint comes from:

- fear
- responsibility
- disgust at appetite disguised as care
- memory of Marcus as dependency made useful

Occasionally:

- a slight crack appears
- a hesitation
- a shift in tone

But she quickly corrects it.

---

### Final Conversation Voice

In the final scene:

- she remains controlled
- but is more present

She does not soften dramatically.

But:

- she listens
- she does not interrupt
- she allows space

Example tone:

> “I didn’t expect you to understand.”  
> “That doesn’t change what I did.”

---

## **Secondary Characters**

### General Guidance

All other characters should:

- feel grounded and distinct
- reflect the norms of the connected world

They should not:

- over-explain connection
- react as if the world is unusual

---

### Key Distinction

The most important variation between characters is:

> how comfortable they are with connection

This should influence:

- pacing of speech
- willingness to share
- emotional exposure

---

## **Silence as Voice**

Silence is an active element.

It can represent:

- respect
- hesitation
- fear
- control

Especially in the final chapter:

> silence is the conclusion

Do not rush it.

Let it sit.

---

## **Final Note**

Every character’s voice should reflect their relationship to:

- connection
- boundaries
- selfhood

If two characters sound interchangeable,

the distinction is not strong enough.

Refine until:

- Rei feels open but evolving
- Yui feels affected and fragile
- Suzu feels controlled and intentional

The reader should be able to identify the speaker

without dialogue tags.
